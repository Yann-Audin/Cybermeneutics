1 Why you should read Philip K. Dick

I am attracted by a specific form of literary brokenness. The kind of brokenness that makes me glad that I don’t have what it takes to be the tortured artist. They are men and women, although historically, societal pressures were so different between the genders that some forms of brokeness are more likely in men than women and vice-versa.

Philip K. Dick is one such author who transformed the misery of life into literary truth: he synthetized mad and maddening wisdoms about reality and published it in silly science-fiction books tjat are only silly because they are classified as science-fiction, and he wrote science-fiction because published only wanted him to write science-fiction. But Dick did not write science-fiction, he wrote reports. If he had not been born in the USA and in the 20s, his work would be studied in the same way as Borges or Sartre. But then, if he had not been born in the USA and in the 20s, Philip K. Dick would not be Philip K. Dick.

Thinking about Dick reminds me of how I think of academia: to give someone the space to think and write makes them a nexus for the societies they inhabit. Dick did not have the space to think and write, but took it nonetheless and became such a nexus for his era. The pressures of the World Wars and the Cold Wars, of the blooming of psychology, the rediscovery of ancient Christian texts, post-war interests in psychoanalysis and esoterism, California’s drug culture, military experiemnts with LSD, the rise of no-fault divorce in the US, the boom of pulp sci-fi, the post-truth mediatic turn, quantum mechanics and cosmology, the space race, the first serious talks of artificial intelligence and androids, the Bomb… Philip K. Dick captured all of it and more.

2 So, Why Philip K. Dick?

Part of it is simply that I grew up reading his stories and still do enjoy them, although that is not enought to warrant a research blog1. Another part of it is my fascination with the literary character that is Philip K. Dick and the improbable life he lead as well as the impact he still has on us to this day. But then again, that does not explain why I should write about him today, because the real reason is deeper and, I am afraid, more sinister. Like Albert Camus, Margaret Atwood, Samuel Beckett, and other literary philosophers, Philip K. Dick doesn’t simply write. He is not bound by aesthetic or storytelling, is main thesis is not political2, nor is it aspirational. No, Philip K. Dick has a philosophy about the nature of reality that encompasses many of the troubles, both social and political, of our current age.

So I ask, in my writing, What is real? Because unceasingly we are bombarded with pseudorealities manufactured by very sophisticated people using very sophisticated electronic mechanisms. I do not distrust their motives; I distrust their power. They have a lot of it. And it is an astonishing power: that of creating whole universes, universes of the mind. I ought to know. I do the same thing. (How To Build A Universe That Doesn’t Fall Apart Two Days Later, Philip K. Dick 1978)

Moreover, the genre of science-fiction allowed him to adress issues that did not even exist during his time that are not central to our current era.

The fact that PKD presented his work as a reflexion on the nature of reality and mankind warrants greater academic attention, which this blog will hopefully contribute to3. But the main and central reason why I personally decided to write about Philip K. Dick is that I was, long ago, infected by his ideas.

To fight the Empire is to be infected by its derangement … Whoever defeats the Empire becomes the Empire; it proliferates like a virus … thereby it becomes its enemies. (Philip K. Dick, Valis 1981)
